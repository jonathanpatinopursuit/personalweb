About Me

I'm a New Yorker who built a career in construction and hospitality, and I'm now bringing that same drive into AI development. Years erecting structural steel as a union ironworker taught me to work with zero margin for error.

Precision isn't optional when the job carries real risk. Before that, running the floor at Cipriani Soho meant thinking fast under pressure while keeping an entire team in sync.

Both jobs put me shoulder-to-shoulder with people who didn't share my background, language, or trade — communicating in English and Spanish across job sites and dining rooms taught me to collaborate with almost anyone and still deliver results. That's exactly the kind of cross-functional teamwork tech companies are looking for.

Outside of work, you'll usually find me riding my single-speed somewhere around the city.

Why Me

I learn technical skills fast, under real stakes, not just in a classroom

When my chemistry degree didn't lead to a job, I didn't retreat to something safe, I went into a union ironworker apprenticeship: structured technical training where mistakes have real consequences. That's the same learning muscle I'm now applying to software, mastering a technical domain from the ground up, fast, with precision built in from day one.

I keep shipping progress when the plan completely breaks, not just when things are going smoothly

A workplace accident ended my ability to do physical labor entirely, not a setback I could work around, a full stop. I didn't spend that time processing, I immediately started retraining for tech through Pursuit. That's evidence I can take a genuine blindside and still produce forward motion within the same window most people would need just to regroup.

I don't wait for direction, I find the next problem and start solving it

Twice now, chemistry to ironworking, then ironworking to tech, I generated my own next move without anyone assigning it to me. On a team, that translates directly: when something stalls or breaks, I'm already working the problem, not waiting to be told to.
